Aim of the project: Investigating the rise of populism in OECD countries

Understanding the evolution of populism and the driving factors behind its increase in developed economies is an important step towards ensuring a more effective governance, and, in turn, a more stable democracy. As shown in chart 1., populism is in an upward trend among OECD countries, starting from 2013, after being relatively stable, low level.
In this project, I provide insights related to this trend through data visualisations and analysis. I explore the geographic distribution of populism, the relationship between populism and government quality, economic factors, and immigration impact. I also discuss the implications of these findings for future policy considerations.

1. The rise of populism

This chart illustrates the evolution of populist discourse in OECD countries from 2000 to 2022, with a projection extending to 2028. Based on speeches from national leaders, the data reveals a compelling story of how populist rhetoric has transformed over two decades. Starting from a relatively low score of 0.10 in 2000, the discourse maintained a stable pattern through the first decade, hovering between 0.15 and 0.20. However, a significant shift occurred around 2012, marking the beginning of a steep upward trajectory.
The most dramatic increase in populist discourse occurred between 2012 and 2020, during which the score more than doubled, ultimately reaching its peak of nearly 0.40 in 2020. This unprecedented surge represents the highest level of populist rhetoric recorded in the measured period. Following this peak, there was a notable decline by 2022, with the score dropping to approximately 0.30, marking the first significant decrease in almost a decade.
Looking toward the future, the projection through 2028 suggests a continued but modest upward trend, as indicated by the dotted line and the accompanying uncertainty range shown in the purple shaded area. The forecast indicates that populist discourse could potentially reach levels between 0.35 and 0.40 by 2028, though with considerable uncertainty as shown by the widening purple band.
This comprehensive analysis, sourced from the Harvard Dataverse Global Populism Database, provides valuable insights into the changing nature of political discourse across OECD nations and suggests that while populist rhetoric has somewhat declined from its 2020 peak, it remains significantly higher than the levels observed at the turn of the millennium.

4. Populism and government quality

Examining the relationship between populist movements and governance effectiveness metrics.
This scatter plot illustrates the relationship between Populism Score and Government Effectiveness across OECD countries from 2000 to 2022, drawing data from both the World Bank and Hawkins et al. (2019) databases.
The x-axis represents the Populism Score, ranging from 0.0 to 1.8, while the y-axis shows Government Effectiveness scores, spanning from -2.0 to 2.5. Each dot on the plot represents a country at a specific point in time, with different colors likely representing different countries or years.
A key feature of the visualization is the dotted blue trend line, which shows a negative correlation between populism and government effectiveness. This suggests that, on average, countries with higher populism scores tend to have lower government effectiveness ratings.
The data points are particularly concentrated in the lower populism score range (0.0-0.4), with government effectiveness scores varying widely in this range from about -1.5 to 2.0. As populism scores increase beyond 0.4, there appears to be both fewer data points and a tendency toward lower government effectiveness scores.
The visualization reveals several interesting patterns: we can observe a dense cluster of observations with populism scores below 0.4 and moderate to high government effectiveness ranging from 0.5 to 2.0. The data becomes more scattered and shows generally lower government effectiveness scores as populism increases. There are some notable outlier cases with high populism scores above 1.0 showing varying levels of government effectiveness. The trend line consistently suggests a gradual decline in government effectiveness as populism increases.
This visualization effectively captures the complex relationship between populist political discourse and the practical effectiveness of governance in OECD nations over more than two decades, suggesting that higher levels of populist rhetoric may be associated with decreased governmental effectiveness.

6. Growth of immigration-related news

This bar chart shows the distribution of migration-related news articles from 2000 to 2024, with data collected from newspaper publications using ProQuest TDM. The y-axis represents the number of articles, ranging from 0 to 4,000, while the x-axis shows the progression of years.
The trend reveals a significant evolution in media coverage of migration over these 24 years. From 2000 to 2012, the number of articles remained relatively stable, fluctuating between 600 and 1,200 articles per year. However, starting from 2013, there's a notable upward trend in coverage.
The most dramatic increase occurs between 2017 and 2018, with the number of articles reaching a peak of approximately 3,600 in 2018. After this peak, there's a slight decline in 2019, followed by another decrease in 2020. The years 2021-2024 show considerable fluctuation, with the most recent data point in 2024 showing around 3,200 articles.
This pattern suggests a significant intensification of media attention to migration issues over time, particularly in the latter half of the 2010s. The sharp increase from 2013 onwards could reflect growing public and media interest in migration issues, possibly coinciding with various global events and policy changes affecting migration patterns.
Notably, the substantial increase in coverage aligns with the timeline we observed in the previous charts showing the rise in populist discourse, suggesting a possible correlation between media coverage of migration and the growth of populist rhetoric in OECD countries.

7. Impact of immigration on populism

This scatter plot illustrates the relationship between Migration News Coverage and Populism scores from 2000-2022, while controlling for GDP. The visualization includes several key elements:
The x-axis shows the Number of Migration News Articles, ranging from 0 to 4,000, while the y-axis displays the Populism Score from 0.14 to 0.40. Each point on the plot is color-coded according to Log(GDP), with a scale ranging from 11.9 (purple) to 12.4 (yellow), as shown in the legend on the right.
A striking feature is the strong positive correlation between migration news coverage and populism scores, indicated by the dotted blue trend line. This relationship is quantified by an R² value of 0.741, suggesting that approximately 74.1% of the variation in populism scores can be explained by the number of migration news articles, when controlling for GDP.
The data points show a clear pattern: as the number of migration news articles increases, there is a corresponding rise in populism scores. The points are relatively tightly clustered around the trend line, particularly in the lower ranges of news coverage (around 800 articles). As the number of articles increases, we see higher populism scores, with some data points showing populism scores above 0.35 when news coverage exceeds 3,000 articles.
The color gradient of the points adds another dimension to the analysis, showing how GDP levels relate to both variables. The variation in colors across the plot suggests that this relationship between migration news coverage and populism exists across different levels of economic development within OECD countries.
This visualization effectively demonstrates a strong statistical relationship between media coverage of migration and the rise of populist discourse, while accounting for economic factors through GDP control.

Source: ProQuest TDM

Data

The analysis draws on four primary data sources to investigate populism in OECD countries from 2000 to 2022. The foundation of the populism measurements comes from Hawkins et al. (2019) Global Populism Database (last updated in 2022), which provides ratings of government leaders' speeches on a scale from 0 (not populist) to 2 (very populist). This dataset focuses specifically on heads of government rather than political parties or other figures, with varying temporal coverage across countries. Download data.
Government effectiveness metrics were sourced from the World Bank's Worldwide Governance Indicators, which measure perceptions of public service quality, civil service independence, policy implementation, and government credibility on a scale from -2.5 to 2.5. Economic data was obtained from the World Bank's World Development Indicators database, specifically focusing on GDP PPP figures for the selected OECD countries. Download Government Effectiveness data.
For media coverage analysis, data was collected using ProQuest TDM to extract news articles from 2000 to 2022 relating to migration topics, including terms like "irregular migrations" and "illegal immigration." All datasets are publicly accessible via GitHub, enabling replication of the analysis. The data processing and visualization were performed using Python, with code available in the project repository for transparency and reproducibility. Download data.

Challenges in data cleaning and analysis

Here is a 200-word overview of the data challenges and solutions encountered during the project: The analysis presented several technical challenges across different visualization stages. For the time series analysis of populism (Chart 1), the primary difficulty lay in standardizing the populism database, which contained multiple speeches per year without specific dates and was organized by leaders rather than countries. This was resolved using pandas DataFrame manipulations to aggregate scores at the country-year level.
The geographical visualizations (Charts 2 and 3) required careful attention to data standardization, particularly in matching ISO3 codes with the Vega topojson file. Additional challenges with Vega's slider functionality were overcome by adapting solutions from previous mapping implementations.
The correlation analyses (Charts 4 and 5) involved merging multiple datasets while maintaining data integrity. A particular challenge arose with y-axis labeling in Vega, which was addressed through alternative visual cues and enhanced tooltips. The migration news analysis (Chart 6) required precise querying in ProQuest TDM to ensure data relevance and reliability.
The final visualization (Chart 7) presented the challenge of effectively displaying three-dimensional relationships. This was resolved by using year as the grouping variable for populism scores and migration news counts, while incorporating GDP data through color-coding based on log values.
All challenges were overcome through a combination of pandas data manipulation, careful database querying, and creative visualization techniques in Vega-Lite.

Conclusion

Summary of findings and implications for future policy considerations.
Based on our comprehensive analysis of populism in OECD countries, several significant insights emerge that help us understand its evolution and driving factors in developed economies.
The temporal analysis reveals a clear inflection point around 2012-2013, after which populist discourse experienced a marked increase from previously stable, low levels. This surge continued until reaching a peak in 2020, followed by a slight decline. The geographical distribution shows considerable variation across OECD nations, with some countries experiencing more dramatic increases than others, particularly in North America and parts of Europe.
A crucial finding emerges from the analysis of government effectiveness and populist discourse. The negative correlation between these variables suggests that higher levels of populism are associated with decreased governmental effectiveness, raising important concerns about the potential impact on democratic institutions and governance quality.
Interestingly, the relationship between populism and economic performance, as measured by GDP, appears more complex and less direct than might be expected. While GDP has shown consistent growth across OECD countries, populist discourse has followed its own distinct pattern, suggesting that economic factors alone do not fully explain the rise in populism.
Perhaps most significantly, our analysis reveals a strong correlation between migration-related news coverage and populist discourse (R² = 0.741, controlling for GDP). The dramatic increase in migration-related news articles from 2013 onwards closely mirrors the rise in populist rhetoric, suggesting that media coverage of migration issues may play a crucial role in shaping populist sentiments.
These findings have important implications for policy considerations and democratic stability. They suggest that addressing the rise of populism requires a nuanced approach that goes beyond purely economic solutions. Special attention should be paid to the role of media coverage in shaping public discourse, the importance of maintaining effective governance structures, and the need to address migration-related concerns in a balanced and constructive manner.
For future policy considerations, these insights suggest the need for: strengthening democratic institutions, developing more effective approaches to migration communication and policy, maintaining focus on government effectiveness, and fostering a more balanced public discourse on complex social issues. This comprehensive understanding of populism's driving factors provides a foundation for developing targeted strategies to ensure more stable and effective democratic governance in OECD nations.
