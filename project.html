Aim of the project: Investigating the rise of populism in OECD countries

The rise of populism in developed economies represents a significant shift in contemporary political dynamics, warranting careful analysis to understand its implications for democratic governance. As demonstrated in Chart 1, populist discourse in OECD countries has shown a marked upward trajectory since 2013, following a period of relative stability at lower levels. This research project examines this trend through comprehensive data analysis and visualization, focusing on several key dimensions: the geographical patterns of populist movements across OECD nations, the interrelationship between populist tendencies and indicators of government effectiveness, the role of economic factors in populist sentiment, and the influence of immigration patterns and related discourse.
Through this analysis, the project aims to provide evidence-based insights to inform policy discussions and contribute to our understanding of how democracies can effectively respond to populist challenges while maintaining institutional stability.

This scatter plot shows the relationship between populism and government effectiveness in OECD countries (2000-2022). A negative correlation emerges with higher populism scores generally associated with lower government effectiveness ratings, particularly above 0.4 on the populism scale.

Data

The analysis draws on four primary data sources to investigate populism in OECD countries from 2000 to 2022. The foundation of the populism measurements comes from Hawkins et al. (2019) Global Populism Database (last updated in 2022), which provides ratings of government leaders' speeches on a scale from 0 (not populist) to 2 (very populist). This dataset focuses specifically on heads of government rather than political parties or other figures, with varying temporal coverage across countries. Download data.
Government effectiveness metrics were sourced from the World Bank's World Development Indicators database. GDP Per Capita growth data across OECD countries was also sourced from the World Bank. Download Government Effectiveness data.
For media coverage analysis, data was collected using ProQuest TDM to extract news articles from 2000 to 2022 relating to migration topics, including terms like "irregular migrations" and "illegal immigration."Download data.
All datasets are publicly accessible via GitHub, enabling replication of the analysis. The data processing and visualization were performed using Python, with code available in the project repository for transparency and reproducibility.

Challenges in data cleaning and analysis

The analysis presented several technical challenges across different visualization stages. For the time series analysis of populism (Chart 1), the primary difficulty lay in standardizing the populism database, which contained multiple speeches per year without specific dates and was organized by leaders rather than countries. This was resolved using pandas DataFrame manipulations to aggregate scores at the country-year level.
The geographical visualizations (Charts 2 and 3) required careful attention to data standardization, particularly in matching ISO3 codes with the Vega topojson file. Additional challenges with Vega's slider functionality were overcome by adapting solutions from previous mapping implementations.
The correlation analyses (Charts 4 and 5) involved merging multiple datasets while maintaining data integrity. A particular challenge arose with y-axis labeling in Vega, which was addressed through alternative visual cues and enhanced tooltips. The migration news analysis (Chart 6) required precise querying in ProQuest TDM to ensure data relevance and reliability.
The final visualization (Chart 7) presented the challenge of effectively displaying three-dimensional relationships. This was resolved by using year as the grouping variable for populism scores and migration news counts, while incorporating GDP data through color-coding based on log values.
All challenges were overcome through a combination of pandas data manipulation, careful database querying, and creative visualization techniques in Vega-Lite.

Conclusion

Our comprehensive analysis of populism in OECD countries reveals complex relationships between political discourse, economic factors, and media coverage from 2000 to 2022. The analysis shows a significant upward trend in populist rhetoric starting from 2013 (Chart 1), with varying intensities across different geographical regions (Charts 2 and 3).
The relationship between populism and governance suggests that higher levels of populist discourse correlate with lower government effectiveness scores (Chart 4). Economic analysis indicates a nuanced temporal relationship between GDP growth and populist trends. While annual GDP growth shows considerable volatility, populist discourse appears to respond to economic challenges with a delay, particularly evident following the 2008 financial crisis and during the pandemic period (Chart 5).
Media coverage analysis reveals a notable correlation with populist discourse. The substantial increase in migration-related news articles (Chart 6) shows a strong statistical relationship with rising populist rhetoric (Chart 7, RÂ²=0.741), while controlling for GDP.
These findings suggest interconnected relationships between economic conditions, media coverage, and populist discourse. The insights provide valuable context for understanding contemporary political dynamics in OECD countries, though further research would be needed to establish causal relationships. This understanding could inform approaches to maintaining effective governance while addressing societal concerns in a balanced manner.
